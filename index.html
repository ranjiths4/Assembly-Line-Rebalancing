IOE 524 Industry Project · Ford Livonia · April 2026

Rebalancing the Ford 8F transmission line to run 1,000 units a day without overtime — by redesigning takt, labor, and station layout.

A volume-down restructuring of Ford Motor Company's 8F automatic transmission assembly system at the Livonia Transmission Plant, spanning six interconnected sub-lines and 171 stations. Audited every station, mapped the full value stream, and designed a phased consolidation path from 131 active stations down to a theoretical 86.

01 · The problem

A line built for high volume, now staring at a 23% demand cut.

The Ford 8F is an eight-speed automatic transmission used across several vehicle platforms. The Livonia line was designed, staffed, and balanced for roughly 1,300 units per day. Shifting market demand pushed the projected daily volume down to about 1,000 — and without restructuring, the line would carry excess labor, idle stations, and flow imbalances that drive up per-unit cost.

System
Six interconnected sub-lines: 905 main assembly, 905 OL offline subassembly, 910 rotating clutch, 907 test & inspection, 935 snap ring & carrier, and 949 MCA. Material flows from inbound storage through subassemblies, into main assembly, through testing, and out to shipment.
Constraint
Target rate of 1,000 units/day on a standard 5-day × 8-hour shift, with zero overtime dependency. Three success criteria: balanced workload across stations, cycle times under the derated takt, and the volume target hit within the standard schedule.
Sponsor
Ford Motor Company — Livonia Transmission Plant. Project conducted under the IOE 524 Industry Project at the University of Michigan with sponsor review at each major checkpoint.
02 · Approach

Five phases — from plant access to validated future state.

Worked station-level rather than line-level. Pulled metrics from Ford's internal monitoring systems, validated each station on the shop floor, then built up the analytics: utilization first, value stream second, future-state design third. No proposal went into the deck without being walked back to a process engineer.

PHASE 01

Baseline & data access

Onboarding, safety training, IT access provisioning, and plant floor walkthroughs to learn the physical layout and material flow paths of the 8F system.

PHASE 02

Station-level data collection

Pulled cycle time, utilization, reject %, starved %, and availability for every station — manual and automated — and validated each against on-line observation. Output: a complete Operation Information Sheet (OIS) covering 171 stations.

PHASE 03

Current-state VSM & constraint identification

Converted the OIS rows into color-coded process blocks (green = manual, pink = automated), connected them in sequence, and mapped buffers between lines. The VSM made interconnection effects visible — including starvation clustering at line transition boundaries.

PHASE 04

Future-state rebalance design

Recalculated takt for the new volume, computed theoretical station count at 85% utilization, identified adjacent manual stations whose combined cycle time fits the new takt ceiling, and flagged low-CT stations as candidates for work redistribution.

PHASE 05

Validation & sponsor review

Reviewed every proposal with Ford process engineers. Incorporated fixed-automation constraints, quality inspection sequencing, ergonomic limits, and physical layout restrictions into the final recommendations.

03 · Analysis

The line wasn't bottlenecked — it was uniformly under-loaded.

No station on the main lines exceeded takt. The problem was uneven loading: some stations at 80%, adjacent ones at 30-50%. That pattern repeated across every sub-line and is the lever the future-state design pulls on.

04 · Future-state design

A phased consolidation built on adjacent-station merges.

The future takt of 28.8 seconds creates a 16.5% headroom over current. Design rule: combined cycle times target 85% of takt — about 24.48 seconds. Merges require manual stations only, physical adjacency, and combined CT within the takt ceiling.

Strategy 01
Recalculate takt & set design target
28.8 sec future takt at 1,000 units/day. Design cycle time: 24.48 sec (85% of takt) to leave a 15% buffer for variability and operator allowances.
Strategy 02
Merge adjacent low-load manual stations
Identified 17 specific pairs across all lines. Eight produce combined CT within takt without process improvement — the lowest-risk Phase 1 starting points.
Strategy 03
Retain automation as backup capacity
Automated stations stay in place even when underutilized — they provide backup during manual station downtime and represent fixed infrastructure.

05 · Deliverables

Five artifacts handed to Ford for Phase 1 rollout.

Each deliverable was reviewed with Ford process engineers. The plan uses a phased rollout — Phase 1 targets the easiest 4 to 8 merges, with a 4-week monitoring period before Phase 2 expansion based on validated performance against the targets in the report.

OIS Station Audit

Operation Information Sheet covering all 171 stations across six sub-lines — cycle time, takt, utilization, reject %, availability, and starved % per station. The single source of truth for every downstream analysis.
